## Literature Review: Exploring the Multifaceted Nature of Workplace Procrastination

Workplace procrastination, the act of delaying or postponing tasks, is a pervasive phenomenon with significant implications for individual well-being and organizational productivity (Pearlmaan-Avnion & Ziibenberg, 2018).  Existing research has explored this complex behavior through various lenses, highlighting the interplay of individual psychological factors, leadership influences, contextual elements, and potential mediating and moderating variables. This literature review synthesizes these diverse perspectives, identifies key findings and gaps in the current understanding, and articulates the novelty of the proposed research.

Early research often focused on the personality underpinnings of procrastination.  Personality traits like conscientiousness and neuroticism have consistently been linked to procrastination tendencies (Gupta et al., 2012).  Individuals low in conscientiousness may struggle with planning and organization, leading to task avoidance, while those high in neuroticism might procrastinate as a coping mechanism for anxiety or fear of failure (Pearlmaan-Avnion & Ziibenberg, 2018).  Furthermore, time perspective, encompassing how individuals perceive and value time, plays a crucial role.  Those with a future-oriented perspective are less likely to procrastinate, while those with a present-oriented view may prioritize immediate gratification over long-term goals (Gupta et al., 2012).  Self-regulation, encompassing self-discipline, goal-setting, and planning abilities, is also a critical factor; individuals with weaker self-regulatory skills are more susceptible to procrastination (Pearlmaan-Avnion & Ziibenberg, 2018).

Beyond individual characteristics, leadership styles have been identified as influential factors in workplace procrastination.  Research suggests that transformational leadership, characterized by inspiring and motivating followers, can mitigate procrastination by fostering a sense of purpose and engagement (Singh & Dhaliwal, 2015).  Conversely, transactional leadership, which relies on rewards and punishments, may inadvertently increase procrastination if it creates a climate of fear or demotivation.  The relationship between leadership and procrastination is likely moderated by job stress, with high levels of stress potentially exacerbating procrastination tendencies in the absence of supportive leadership.

The mediating role of creativity has emerged as a significant area of inquiry.  Shin and Grant (2021) propose a curvilinear relationship between procrastination and creativity, suggesting that moderate levels of procrastination can actually enhance creative problem-solving by allowing for incubation and divergent thinking.  This implies that procrastination isn't always detrimental; it can provide opportunities for novel insights.  Furthermore, problem restructurering, the way individuals approach and redefine challenges, acts as a mediator between job stress and procrastination.  Individuals who are adept at reframing problems may be less likely to procrastinate even under high stress conditions.

Finally, the moderating role of anxiety regulation has gained increasing attention.  Pearlmaan-Avnion and Ziibenberg (2018) highlight the importance of an individual's ability to manage and control anxiety levels in mitigating the negative impact of personality traits on procrastination.  Effective anxiety regulation can buffer the tendency to procrastinate, allowing individuals to engage with tasks despite feelings of apprehension.

**Gap in the Literature and Novelty of the Proposed Research:**

While existing research has explored these individual, leadership, and contextual factors, a comprehensive understanding of their *interactive* effects on workplace procrastination remains limited.  Most studies examine these variables in isolation or in pairwise relationships.  The proposed research aims to address this gap by employing a mixed-methods approach to investigate the *combined* influence of personality traits, time perspective, self-regulation, leadership styles, job stress, and anxiety regulation on procrastination, while also exploring the mediating role of creativity and problem restructurering.  Furthermore, the potential moderating effect of anxiety regulation on the relationship between personality traits and procrastination will be investigated.  This integrated approach promises to provide a more nuanced and realistic understanding of the complex interplay of factors contributing to workplace procrastination, moving beyond simple correlations to explore more intricate causal pathways.  The inclusion of qualitative follow-up interviews will allow for a deeper contextualization of the quantitative findings, providing richer insights into the lived experiences of individuals struggling with procrastination in diverse work environments.  This comprehensive investigation will contribute to a more holistic and practical understanding of workplace procrastination, with implications for developing targeted interventions to enhance individual productivity and organizational well-being.
